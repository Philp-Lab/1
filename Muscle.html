Muscle

Our lab is focused on researching ways to help prevent the decline in muscle mass and function often seen with ageing or periods of inactivity. 

LEARN MORE

Muscle wasting, or muscle atrophy, is a widespread problem espcially in elderly populations. A reduction in the amount or functionality of muscle greatly increases the chances of falls and of developing metabolic diseases such as type 2 diabetes.

Research groups like ours, are currently searching for novel, safe, easy-to-use nutritional therapies to promote hypertrophy and/ or reduce muscle atrophy.

Currently, the best-known methods to increase muscle size and strength are protein feeding and resistance exercise training.

Performing regular resistance exercise is a potent and necessary stimulus for muscle growth, and provision of protein in the diet can help to maximise this growth in both younger and older adults.

Resistance exercise exerts this effect by promoting the synthesis of new muscle proteins (building blocks for muscle) and reducing the breakdown of muscle proteins. However, the effect of resistance exercise and protein feeding is significantly blunted as we age, such that more exercise and more protein is required to maintain muscle mass during ageing. Therefore, the search for novel methods to maintain muscle size is underway 

Muscle atrophy is not only a concern for the elderly.
Prolongued periods of inactivity or muscle disuse is often unavoidable such as following surgery or recovery from illness. These periods of disuse also lead to a wasting away of the muscle. For instance even in yound healthy men, just one week of bed rest resulted in a loss of 1.4 ± 0.2 kg lean tissue loss and a 3.2 ± 0.9% decline in quadriceps muscle cross sectional area (Diabetes. 2016 Oct;65(10):2862-75. doi: 10.2337/db15-1661. Epub 2016 Jun 29.)
